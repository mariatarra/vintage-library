Four writers who dared to explore the darker reaches of human nature

Author Profiles

Mary Shelley

1797 – 1851 • British

Born Mary Wollstonecraft Godwin, she was the daughter of political philosopher William Godwin and feminist writer Mary Wollstonecraft. She began writing Frankenstein at the age of eighteen during a summer stay at the Villa Diodati near Lake Geneva, as part of a ghost story competition with Lord Byron and Percy Bysshe Shelley, whom she later married.

Her novel was published anonymously in 1818 and is now considered a foundational text of both Gothic and science fiction literature. Shelley went on to write several other novels, including The Last Man (1826), though none matched the enduring impact of Frankenstein.

Oscar Wilde

1854 – 1900 • Irish

Oscar Fingal O'Flahertie Wills Wilde was one of the most celebrated playwrights and wits of the Victorian era. Born in Dublin and educated at Oxford, Wilde became the leading proponent of Aestheticism — the philosophy that art exists for its own sake, divorced from moral purpose. His plays, essays, and only novel cemented his reputation as one of the great stylists of the English language.

His life ended in tragedy. Convicted of gross indecency in 1895, he was sentenced to two years of hard labour. He died in Paris in 1900, aged 46, impoverished and in exile. His works have since been fully rehabilitated and are now considered cornerstones of English literature.

Fyodor Dostoevsky

1821 – 1881 • Russian

Dostoevsky is considered one of the supreme masters of world literature. His life was marked by extraordinary hardship: he was arrested for alleged revolutionary activities, sentenced to death, reprieved at the last moment before a firing squad, and sent to a Siberian labour camp for four years. These experiences profoundly shaped his understanding of suffering, faith, and human nature.

His major novels — Crime and Punishment, The Idiot, Demons, and The Brothers Karamazov — are landmarks of psychological and philosophical fiction. Nietzsche, Freud, and Einstein all cited him as a profound influence.

→ Read The Brothers Karamazov

Matthew Lewis

1775 – 1818 • British

Matthew Gregory Lewis wrote The Monk in ten weeks at the age of nineteen while working as an attaché at the British Embassy in The Hague. The novel caused an immediate scandal upon publication in 1796, condemned by critics for its explicit content and blasphemous themes. Lewis was dubbed "Monk" Lewis for the rest of his life.

Despite the controversy — or perhaps because of it — The Monk became enormously influential on subsequent Gothic writers including Ann Radcliffe, Percy Shelley, and later Edgar Allan Poe. Lewis went on to write plays and poetry but never again matched the notoriety of his debut.
